A great marketing manager isn't just someone with a polished portfolio and a long list of campaigns. The right hire knows how to set strategy, measure what matters, lead a team without micromanaging, and tell the difference between vanity metrics and revenue impact. This guide gives you 40 questions across six categories, plus exactly what to listen for in each answer.

## How to use this guide

A solid marketing manager interview loop is 6–8 questions across 2–3 rounds. Don't ask all 40. Pick a mix that maps to what you actually need: 1–2 strategy questions, 1–2 campaign execution, 1–2 analytics and measurement, 1 leadership or collaboration, 1–2 behavioral, and 1 culture or motivation.

For each answer, listen for specifics. Vague answers ("we drove a lot of engagement") are the biggest red flag in marketing interviews. Strong candidates name numbers, name channels, and name what didn't work.

## Strategy and planning (questions 1–8)

These reveal whether the candidate thinks in systems or just executes tactics.

**1. How would you build a marketing strategy for our product from scratch?** **Listen for:** clarifying questions about audience, budget, and goals before answering. A candidate who jumps straight to "I'd run Google Ads" is showing tactics, not strategy.

**2. How do you prioritize channels when you have a limited budget?** **Listen for:** framework thinking — payback period, CAC by channel, lifecycle alignment. Watch for candidates who default to "test everything."

**3. Walk me through how you'd position a new product in a crowded market.** **Listen for:** awareness of category alternatives, jobs-to-be-done thinking, willingness to pick a sharp wedge instead of "we do everything better."

**4. How do you balance brand marketing with performance marketing?** **Listen for:** understanding that both matter, with specific examples of how they've shifted spend based on funnel pressure or saturation.

**5. What does a good marketing plan actually look like?** **Listen for:** goals tied to revenue, channel mix with rationale, measurement plan, and a budget that maps to outcomes. Watch for plans that are all activities, no outcomes.

**6. How do you decide whether to chase a new channel like TikTok or LinkedIn?** **Listen for:** audience research, small test budget, clear kill criteria. Strong candidates know how to fail fast.

**7. What's your approach to competitive analysis?** **Listen for:** ongoing process (not a one-time deck), specific tools they use, and how findings change strategy. "I follow them on LinkedIn" is not enough.

**8. How do you align marketing strategy with sales goals?** **Listen for:** lead handoff process, SLAs, joint pipeline reviews. Watch for candidates who treat sales as an afterthought.

## Campaign execution (questions 9–15)

The strategy questions test thinking. These test whether they can ship.

**9. Walk me through your most successful campaign.** **Listen for:** the goal, the constraints, what they actually did, specific results with numbers, and what they'd do differently. Generic answers mean they didn't own it.

**10. Tell me about a campaign that flopped.** **Listen for:** honesty, root cause analysis, what they learned. Candidates who never had a flop either didn't ship much or aren't being honest.

**11. How do you scope a campaign from kickoff to launch?** **Listen for:** timeline, dependencies, stakeholders, creative review process. Watch for candidates who skip operational details.

**12. How do you brief a designer or copywriter?** **Listen for:** clarity on objective, audience, tone, must-haves, and constraints. Bonus: examples of good briefs they've written.

**13. Walk me through a multi-channel campaign you've run.** **Listen for:** consistency across touchpoints, sequencing, attribution approach, and team coordination. Single-channel candidates may not be ready for manager-level.

**14. How do you handle last-minute campaign changes from leadership?** **Listen for:** pushback when it's warranted, fast pivots when it's not, communication with the team. "I just do what they say" is a soft no.

**15. What's your approach to A/B testing?** **Listen for:** statistical rigor (or honest acknowledgement of small-sample limits), one variable at a time, clear hypothesis. Watch for candidates who run tests they can't read.

## Analytics and measurement (questions 16–22)

These separate operators from order-takers. Strong marketing managers can argue with data — and know when not to.

**16. What metrics matter most for this role?** **Listen for:** metrics tied to revenue or pipeline, not just impressions and reach. Bonus: they ask clarifying questions about your business model first.

**17. How do you measure brand marketing?** **Listen for:** awareness studies, branded search lift, organic traffic trends, share of voice. Honest acknowledgement that brand is harder to measure but still measurable.

**18. Tell me about a time data changed your mind about a campaign.** **Listen for:** specific data, what changed, and what they did about it. Watch for candidates who can't recall a single example.

**19. How do you handle attribution?** **Listen for:** awareness that no model is perfect, multi-touch thinking, comfort with directional data. Candidates who claim perfect attribution are bluffing.

**20. What's your stance on vanity metrics?** **Listen for:** clear definition (likes, impressions, follower counts without context), when they still matter (top-of-funnel signals), and how they push back on leadership asking for them.

**21. How do you build a marketing dashboard?** **Listen for:** metrics that drive decisions, not metrics that look impressive. Bonus: tools they use (GA4, Looker, Mixpanel) and how often the team reviews them.

**22. What would you do if your CEO asked for a metric you don't believe in?** **Listen for:** respectful pushback with alternatives, willingness to deliver while explaining the limitation. Pure compliance or pure refusal are both red flags.

## Leadership and team collaboration (questions 23–28)

For manager-level roles, this is where you separate doers from leaders.

**23. How do you manage a marketing team with different skill sets — content, paid, design?** **Listen for:** respect for specialists, clear ownership, regular 1:1s, shared goals. Watch for candidates who treat the team as interchangeable.

**24. Tell me about a time you had to give difficult feedback to a direct report.** **Listen for:** specifics, timeliness, what changed afterward. Vague answers mean they avoided the conversation.

**25. How do you collaborate with sales, product, and engineering?** **Listen for:** concrete rituals (joint reviews, shared docs, weekly syncs), examples of conflict resolution. "We communicate well" is not a process.

**26. How do you decide what to do in-house vs hire an agency?** **Listen for:** core competencies stay in-house, specialized/short-term goes to agencies, awareness of management overhead. Strong opinions either way are fine if reasoned.

**27. Describe how you've hired or grown a team.** **Listen for:** hiring loops they've designed, onboarding plans, how they've grown junior reports. Manager-level signal.

**28. What's your approach to managing freelancers and contractors?** **Listen for:** clear scope, deliverables-based payment, communication cadence, and what they do when work falls short.

## Behavioral (questions 29–35)

Use the STAR method (Situation, Task, Action, Result) as your listening framework.

**29. Tell me about a time you disagreed with your CMO or VP.** **Listen for:** data-driven argument, professional handling, willingness to disagree and commit afterward. Red flag: stories where they were always right.

**30. Describe a time you had to do a lot with a small budget.** **Listen for:** creativity, prioritization, and specific outcomes. Strong candidates love this question.

**31. Tell me about a time you missed a goal.** **Listen for:** honesty, root cause analysis, what they changed afterward. Marketing managers who never miss either don't set hard goals or don't tell you about misses.

**32. Describe a situation where you had to influence without authority.** **Listen for:** relationship-building, data-driven persuasion, patience. This is the most common reality of cross-functional marketing work.

**33. Tell me about a campaign where stakeholders disagreed.** **Listen for:** how they ran the discussion, where they pushed back, where they compromised. Watch for candidates who steamroll or capitulate.

**34. Describe a time you had to learn a new channel or tool quickly.** **Listen for:** learning approach, who they leaned on, how they validated they'd learned it. Adaptability is a core marketing trait.

**35. Tell me about the hardest decision you've made as a marketer.** **Listen for:** specificity, trade-offs they weighed, what they'd do differently. Avoid candidates whose hardest decision was picking a font.

## Culture, growth, and motivation (questions 36–40)

These reveal whether the candidate actually wants this job, or whether they're shopping the next title.

**36. Why do you want to work here specifically?** **Listen for:** specific knowledge of your product, market, or team. Generic answers are a soft no.

**37. What kind of marketing culture do you do your best work in?** **Listen for:** honest fit with how your team actually operates. Mismatch here costs you in month three.

**38. Where do you want to be in three years?** **Listen for:** ambition that fits the role, alignment with what your team can offer. A candidate who wants to be CMO in 18 months won't stay.

**39. How do you stay current with marketing trends?** **Listen for:** specific newsletters, podcasts, communities, and a habit of testing what they read. Watch for candidates who name-drop without depth.

**40. What questions do you have for us?** **Listen for:** thoughtful questions about strategy, team dynamics, success metrics. No questions is a strong negative.

## How to score marketing manager candidates

Use a simple rubric across five dimensions, each rated 1–4: **strategic thinking** (do they reason about trade-offs and channel mix?), **execution** (can they ship campaigns end-to-end without hand-holding?), **data fluency** (are they comfortable with metrics and honest about limits?), **leadership** (can they grow a team and collaborate cross-functionally?), and **culture fit** (will they thrive in how your team actually operates?).

Calibrate scores across interviewers after every loop. A 4 in strategy with a 2 in execution is a very different hire than a 3 across the board.

## Frequently asked questions

### How many questions should a marketing manager interview include?

Aim for 6–8 well-chosen questions across a 2–3 round loop. Trying to cover 15 in one round produces shallow signal. Depth beats breadth.

### What's the difference between interviewing a marketing manager and a senior marketer?

Manager-level interviews weight leadership, team-building, and cross-functional collaboration more heavily. Senior individual contributor interviews focus on execution depth and craft. Don't ask the same questions for both.

### How do I tell if a candidate is exaggerating their results?

Ask specific follow-ups about their personal contribution. "What part of that campaign did you own end-to-end?" or "What would have been different if you weren't there?" Candidates who took credit for team work struggle here.

### Should I ask marketing managers to do a case study?

Yes, for senior roles. A short take-home (4–6 hours max) or a live whiteboard exercise reveals strategic thinking better than any verbal answer. Pay them for take-home work above 2 hours.
